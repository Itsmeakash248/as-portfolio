Chapter 5

Correlation and Regression

Correlation quantifies relationship strength and direction, while regression builds equations for prediction and estimation between variables.

1. Concept of Correlation

Correlation measures how two variables move together. Positive correlation means both increase together, negative means one increases as the other decreases.

  • Simple correlation: between two variables.
  • Multiple correlation: one variable vs a group.
  • Partial correlation: relationship after controlling others.

2. Scatter Diagram Method

A scatter plot provides immediate visual evidence of direction and linearity before formal coefficient computation.

  • Upward slope: positive relationship.
  • Downward slope: negative relationship.
  • No pattern: weak or no relationship.

3. Pearson and Spearman

Pearson r = covariance(X, Y) / (sigmaX * sigmaY)
Spearman rho = 1 - [6 * sum(d^2)] / [n * (n^2 - 1)]

Pearson is used for interval data; Spearman is useful for ranked or ordinal data.

Regression Analysis

Regression extends relationship analysis into prediction.

4. Regression Lines

  • Regression of Y on X predicts Y from X.
  • Regression of X on Y predicts X from Y.
  • Both lines intersect at mean point (x-bar, y-bar).
Y - y-bar = b_yx (X - x-bar)
X - x-bar = b_xy (Y - y-bar)

5. Regression Coefficients

Coefficients b_yx and b_xy describe expected change in one variable per unit change in the other. Both share the same sign as correlation.

r^2 = b_yx * b_xy

6. Reliability and Use Cases

  • Probable error evaluates reliability of observed correlation.
  • Regression supports forecasting in business and economics.
  • Useful in trend analysis, cost modeling, and performance prediction.
Learning Outcomes Interpret relationship direction and strength, compute and compare Pearson and Spearman coefficients, derive regression equations, and use fitted models for practical prediction tasks.
