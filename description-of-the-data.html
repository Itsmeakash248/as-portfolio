Fundamental Concepts

Understanding structure before computation improves accuracy and interpretation.

1. Types of Data

Qualitative data captures categories and labels, while quantitative data represents measurable numeric values. Quantitative data can be discrete (countable) or continuous (measurable on a range).

  • Qualitative: gender, color, city.
  • Discrete quantitative: number of students, number of books.
  • Continuous quantitative: height, weight, temperature.

2. Scales of Measurement

Measurement scales define valid operations and interpretation depth.

  • Nominal: category labels with no ranking.
  • Ordinal: ordered categories with unequal intervals.
  • Interval: equal differences without a true zero.
  • Ratio: equal differences with an absolute zero.

3. Frequency Distribution

Frequency tables condense large datasets into readable form. Discrete tables list exact values; continuous tables group values into class intervals.

Marks interval Frequency Cumulative frequency
0 - 10 4 4
10 - 20 6 10
20 - 30 5 15

4. Measures of Central Tendency

Central tendency identifies the representative value of a distribution.

Mean = (sum of observations) / (number of observations)
  • Mean is sensitive to extreme values.
  • Median is robust for skewed distributions.
  • Mode highlights the most frequent category or value.

5. Measures of Dispersion

Dispersion quantifies spread and reliability. Two datasets may share the same mean but differ in variability.

Range = Maximum - Minimum
Standard deviation = square root of variance
Coefficient of variation = (SD / Mean) * 100

6. Shape of Distribution

Skewness and kurtosis explain distribution symmetry and tail behavior.

  • Positive skew: tail extends right, mean > median.
  • Negative skew: tail extends left, mean < median.
  • Symmetric: mean approximately equals median approximately equals mode.
Learning Outcomes Organize raw data systematically, compute descriptive measures, interpret graphs such as histograms and boxplots, and prepare datasets for higher-level statistical inference.
